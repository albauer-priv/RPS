# Evidence Layer
## Scientific & Practice-Based Foundations for Durability-First Ultra-Endurance Training

**Purpose**  
This document provides the **scientific and practice-based evidence base** that underpins the
Principles Paper, Agent Instruction, and all downstream planning decisions.

It answers the question:

> *Why are these principles valid – and who supports them?*

This document:
- contains **no rules, no thresholds, no prescriptions**
- does **not override governance**
- exists solely to **justify and explain** decisions

---

## 1. Durability & Fatigue Resistance as a Core Performance Dimension

### Stephen Seiler

**Core contribution**
- Introduced **Durability** as an independent determinant of endurance performance
- Defined durability practically as the **onset and magnitude of physiological drift** (HR, RPE, efficiency) during prolonged submaximal work

**Key implications**
- Peak metrics (FTP, VO₂max) explain performance only when fresh
- Ultra- and marathon events are decided by **how late degradation begins**
- Training must therefore target *stability under fatigue*, not only capacity

**Key sources**
- Seiler – *Durability: The Fourth Pillar of Endurance Performance* (Fast Talk)
- Seiler Q&A – Durability & High-Intensity Repeatability

Recent field-based evidence further supports durability as an independent performance dimension.  
Barsumyan et al. (2025) demonstrated that performance after accumulated work explains race outcomes beyond VO₂max, FTP, or critical power, reinforcing durability as a standalone determinant rather than a derivative of peak capacity.


---

### Peter Leo (INSCYD, JSC)

**Core contribution**
- Operationalized durability via **performance under energetic pre-load**
- Emphasized **kJ/kg and cumulative work** as prerequisites for valid durability assessment

**Key implications**
- Performance tests without sufficient prior work are **not durability tests**
- Energy system behavior (VLamax, substrate use) shifts under fatigue
- Training and evaluation must reflect **energetic reality of events**

**Key sources**
- Leo et al. – *Durability as an independent parameter of endurance performance* (Journal of Science and Cycling)
- INSCYD practitioner publications

---

Recent conceptual work further differentiates durability from related fatigue constructs.  
Meixner et al. (2025) distinguish durability from fatigability, repeatability, and resilience, highlighting that training improving repeatability or short-term fatigue tolerance does not automatically enhance durability under prolonged load.


---

### Paul Larsen

**Core contribution**
- Ultra-endurance case studies emphasizing **robustness over peak performance**
- Focus on **training density, repeatability, and resilience** rather than maximal metrics

**Key implications**
- Successful ultra athletes often display unremarkable peak values
- Long-term consistency and tolerance to high training load dominate outcomes

**Key sources**
- Larsen – Ultra-endurance performance case studies
- Applied physiology lectures & podcasts

## 2. Intensity Distribution & Aerobic Base

### Polarized / Pyramidal Training (Seiler)

**Core findings**
- ~75–90% of training time at low intensity optimizes long-term adaptation
- Clear separation of low and high intensity preserves quality and recovery

**Relevance for ultra-distance**
- High LIT volume improves mitochondrial density and fatigue resistance
- Excessive "moderate" work accelerates drift and undermines durability

---

### San Millán & Metabolic Efficiency

**Core contribution**
- Demonstrated the importance of **mitochondrial function and FATmax**
- Linked low-intensity volume to metabolic efficiency and endurance robustness

**Implication**
- Z2 is not just recovery – it is a **primary adaptation driver** for ultra athletes

---

## 3. High-Intensity Training & VO₂max

### Rønnestad & Hansen

**Core findings**
- Structured VO₂max intervals significantly raise aerobic ceiling
- Effective doses are **limited** and show diminishing returns

**Implications**
- VO₂max is best treated as a **supporting capacity**, not the main focus
- Overuse of HIIT compromises recovery and long-duration consistency

---

### Empirical Cycling / CTS (Applied Interpretation)

**Key points**
- VO₂ work improves repeatability and fatigue tolerance when dosed conservatively
- High-intensity density must decrease as volume and durability demands rise

---

Methodological reviews underline the heterogeneity of durability assessment.  
Peeters et al. (2025) show that durability outcomes depend strongly on preload design (duration, intensity, kJ), highlighting the need for consistent protocols when using durability metrics longitudinally.

---

## 4. Energetics, kJ-Based Load & Fueling

### CTS – Time-Crunched Cyclist Podcast (#200, #208)

**Core contribution**
- Positioned **kilojoules** as the unifying metric linking:
  - training load
  - fueling requirements
  - body composition management

**Implications**
- kJ enable sport-specific, duration-relevant load planning
- Fueling should scale with **energetic rate**, not abstract zones

---

### Leo / San Millán – Substrate Use

**Key points**
- Lower VLamax and higher fat oxidation reduce carbohydrate drain
- Efficient metabolism supports durability under prolonged stress

---

## 5. Durability-Specific Training Methods

### Hard-Late & Back-to-Back Training

**Evidence base**
- Seiler & Leo: adaptations are stimulus- and context-dependent
- Fatigue-resistance improves when quality is trained **after pre-load**

**Implication**
- Late-session tempo, long Z2, and B2B rides target durability directly


Experimental work shows that accumulated work is not physiologically equivalent across intensity distributions.  
Mateo-March et al. (2024) demonstrated task-specific durability effects depending on preload intensity, reinforcing that durability is context-dependent rather than a simple function of total work.


---

## 6. Masters Athletes (50+)

**Consensus across authors**
- Reduced recovery capacity
- Higher injury risk under aggressive intensity density

**Implications**
- Lower intensity frequency
- Greater emphasis on durability, not peak power
- Conservative progression is not optional
